# Writing That Works

A playbook for business writing that earns action. The goal is never just clarity — it is **effective communication that produces a result**: an approval, a yes, a hire, a check, a decision, a behaviour change. Every guideline below serves that goal.

## When to use this skill

Use it for any of these formats:
- Email (especially to busy or senior readers)
- Memos and business letters
- Presentation decks and speeches
- Plans, reports, annual letters
- Recommendations, proposals, grant applications
- Sales letters, fund-raising letters
- Resumes and cover letters
- Editing any of the above

Do **not** use it for: tweets/X posts, blog drafts in a personal voice, fiction, UX microcopy, technical API docs. Those have different conventions.

## The first question, always

Before drafting, force an answer to: **What do I want the reader to do?** Then the three most important things they need to understand to take that action. *Then* start writing. If you can't answer that, the writing isn't ready to start.

When done, re-read as the reader. Would you act on what's written?

## Part 1 — The 18 principles (the heart of the book)

These apply to almost everything. Internalise them.

### 1. Don't mumble
Once you know what you want to say, come right out and say it. Tiptoeing tempts the reader to tune out.

> "It is generally desirable to communicate your thoughts in a forthright manner." → **"Don't mumble."**

### 2. Make the organisation clear
For anything longer than a few paragraphs, tell the reader where you're going. Outline first. Use numbered/underlined headings as signposts. End with a summary — and a summary is *not* a conclusion; it restates points already made.

### 3. Short paragraphs, short sentences, short words
Model the front page of the *Wall Street Journal* — first paragraphs are rarely more than three sentences; many are one. Prefer the short word to the long one when meaning is the same:

| Prefer | Over |
|---|---|
| Now | Currently |
| Start | Initiate |
| Show | Indicate |
| Finish | Finalize |
| Speed up | Expedite |
| Use | Utilize |
| Place, put | Position |

Long words are often a sign the writer hasn't worked out what they want to say.

### 4. Active and personal
Active voice over passive. It reveals the actor and adds energy.

- *It is recommended* → **We recommend**
- *He should be told* → **Get Alice to tell him**
- *Personal sacrifices are being made* → **We see people making sacrifices**

### 5. Avoid vague adjectives and adverbs
Don't say "very overspent" — say **"overspent by $10,000."** Don't say "slightly behind schedule" — say **"one day late."** Vigorous modifiers ("instantly accepted," "moist handshake," "tiresome speech") sharpen; lazy ones ("very good," "awfully nice," "basically accurate") just inflate.

### 6. Down-to-earth language — no jargon
Strip buzzwords. Translate as you draft:

| Buzzword | Plain English |
|---|---|
| Interface | Discuss, meet, work with |
| Impact (v.) | Affect |
| Resource-constrained | Not enough people/money |
| Incent | Motivate |
| Skill set | Skills |
| Net net | Conclusion |
| Suboptimal | Less than ideal |
| Push the envelope | Test the limits |
| Bandwidth | Time, or ability |
| Operationalize | Achieve |
| Bake in | Include |
| Drill down | Analyze |
| On a go-forward basis | In the future |

If you find yourself stacking these ("meaningful interface with management may be necessitated"), rewrite as plain English ("we'd better ask management to listen to our case").

### 7. Be specific
Generalities ("a great success", "from more places than ever before") let skeptical readers stay uninformed. Specifics ("enrollment doubled to 560; students came from twenty-seven states, Germany, and Canada") prove the case.

### 8. Choose the right word
Pairs that matter:
- **affect** (verb, influence) / **effect** (noun: result; verb: bring about)
- **it's** (it is) / **its** (possessive — no apostrophe)
- **i.e.** (that is) / **e.g.** (for example)
- **principal** (first in rank) / **principle** (a guiding rule)
- **imply** (suggest) / **infer** (deduce)
- **mitigate** (lessen) / **militate** (have force as evidence against)
- **disinterested** (neutral) / **uninterested** (doesn't care)
- **fortunate** (lucky) / **fortuitous** (by chance)
- **alternate** (back and forth) / **alternative** (a choice)
- **definite** (certain) / **definitive** (authoritative, final)
- **fulsome** (excessive, insincere — NOT "full and abundant")
- **notable** (worth noting) / **notorious** (famous in a bad way)

Confusing these signals illiteracy to the readers most likely to count.

### 9. Make it perfect
No typos, no misspellings, no errors in numbers or dates. A single error makes readers question how much care went into the whole. Spell-check is fallible (it would happily turn "Stanley Pigott" into "Stanley piglet"); proofread on paper if it matters.

### 10. Come to the point
Churchill didn't write *"The situation in regard to France is very serious"* — he wrote **"The news from France is bad."** The Gettysburg Address is 266 words. The shortest sentence in the New Testament — "Jesus wept." — is the most moving.

> "I would have written you a shorter letter, but I didn't have the time." (often attributed to Pascal)

### 11. Write the way you talk
A spoken message of ten words ("Ben, if you need more manuals, just ask for them") balloons in writing into twenty-four words of pomposity ("Should the supply of manuals sent you not be sufficient..."). Test every sentence: *would I say this to my reader's face?* If no, rewrite.

### 12. Strike out words you don't need

| Don't write | Write |
|---|---|
| Advance plan | Plan |
| Take action | Act |
| Hold a meeting | Meet |
| Study in depth | Study |
| New innovations | Innovations |
| Consensus of opinion | Consensus |
| At this point in time | Now |
| Until such time as | Until |
| In the majority of instances | Usually |
| On a local basis | Locally |
| Basically unaware of | Did not know |
| The overall plan | The plan |
| With regard to | About |
| In view of, on the basis of | Because |
| In the event of | If |
| For the purpose of | To |
| Despite the fact that | Although |
| Inasmuch as | Since |

### 13. Use standard English
Don't pioneer new usage in formal writing. Don't use "like" where "as if" or "as though" fits. Don't write "He asked Helen and I" — try the pronoun alone ("He asked I"?). Established usage offends nobody.

### 14. Don't write like a lawyer or bureaucrat
Long sentences, clauses-within-clauses, gratuitous Latin ("re:"), and dense legalese. If you catch yourself writing this way, draft as you'd say it face-to-face, then adjust the formality up only as much as needed.

### 15. Keep in mind what your reader doesn't know
Define abbreviations on first use. Note that "K" means thousand here, "M" means million in the UK; 9/12 is September 12 in the US, December 9 in Europe.

### 16. Punctuate carefully
"Woman without her man has no reason for living" → with punctuation: "Woman: without her, man has no reason for living." Don't use quotation marks for emphasis — they suggest the opposite. ("This bolt provides 'superior' tensile strength" reads as a hedge.)

### 17. Understate, don't overstate
A single obvious exaggeration arouses suspicion of the whole case. Round numbers conservatively: 6.7 is "over six and a half," not "nearly seven." H.L. Mencken's last instruction to his obituary writer: *"Don't overdo it."*

### 18. Write so you cannot be misunderstood
Careful writers are alert to every way they might be misread. Break overloaded sentences into two. Test technical material on a lay reader before sending.

## Part 2 — Format-specific playbooks

### E-mail

Most senior readers get 100+ messages a day and delete or ignore most of them. The job is to *get read and acted on*.

1. **Subject line does the work.** No subject = unopened. Make it specific and compelling: *"Winning client approval"* beats *"Status of proposal."* Don't overuse URGENT — it stops working.
2. **Cut ruthlessly.** Anything longer than one screen risks not being read; send long material as an attachment. Try removing 50% of your draft.
3. **Avoid email tag.** Set context: "I'm available to speak at your meeting on the 14th" beats "I'm available." State what you want the reader to do and a deadline if you need a response.
4. **Set tone deliberately.** No voice, no face — terse questions read as angry. A greeting (*Hello, George*) and a sign-off (*Thanks*, *Best*) prevent misreads. Compose yourself before composing the message; sleep on anything written in anger.
5. **Reply All is dangerous.** Count to ten. List one name in TO: when you want action — multiple recipients diffuses responsibility.
6. **Nothing is private.** Office email belongs to the company. Never put anything personally negative, distasteful, or that you wouldn't want on the front page of the *Times*.
7. **Use email + voice mail together** for important matters: a brief voice mail alerting the recipient that an email is coming.

### Memos and letters

- **Title the memo** in caps, centred: `RAISE FOR TONY ANDRINO`, not `LONG OVERDUE`. Easy to spot in a file.
- **Address only the actor**, cc those who need to know. List cc names alphabetically (except where rank makes that ludicrous).
- **Structure visibly** for anything over half a page. Outline first.
- **Letters**: get the name right (spelling, gender — Mickey, Terry, Sandy go either way). Use "Dear Joan Larson" when you've met but aren't on first-name terms. Make the first sentence work hard — identify the subject at once. Stop when you're through (don't pad with "Please call if you have any questions"). State the next step specifically: "Please let us know by August 1" beats "Hoping to hear soon."

**Letter that says no:** Be as clear, tactful, and understanding as you would face-to-face. Anticipate the reader's questions. Avoid "We regret to inform you that..." — nobody talks that way. Try: "I know you'll be disappointed, but there isn't a way I can do that — though here's an alternative."

**Letter that asks for something:** Say what you want, *then* who you are and why, *then* thanks. Don't open with appreciation.

### Presentations and decks

- **Start with a written objective and a one-line theme.** Put the theme on a chart that stays visible throughout.
- **Show an agenda.** Estimate time on the long side — a 20-minute talk that runs 25 feels eternal; a 30-minute talk that runs 25 feels crisp.
- **Talk about *them*, not about us.** The biggest mistake is opening with your credentials. Start with the audience's market, problem, or need. Credentials can come at the end and often aren't needed.
- **Headlines, not labels.** Chart titles should make the point: *"Low price competition is gaining"* beats *"Trends."*
- **Read every word on the slide aloud.** Don't paraphrase — the audience will read what's in front of them and you'll be competing with your own slides.
- **Face the audience.** Avoid dark rooms with slides, especially after lunch.
- **Pyramid Principle** (McKinsey/Minto): every point at one level summarises the level below; ideas group horizontally because they share a logical argument. Standard deck flow: *Objective → Background → Facts → Conclusions → Recommendations → Next steps.*
- **Finish strong** with a clear ask or next action.

### Speeches

1. **Frame with a strong point of view.** A good title reflects it ("A Healthy Respect for Uncertainty"). Lazy title = no point of view.
2. **Start fast.** Skip "It's an honor and a privilege." Plunge in.
3. **Write to be spoken.** Read aloud, edit until it sounds like you. Never deliver a ghostwritten speech without making it sound like you.
4. **Leave them thinking.** Best speeches inspire the audience to see the subject from a fresh angle.
5. **No speech was ever too short.** Most good talks run under 20 minutes. You will not regret cutting.
6. **Rehearse until you almost know it by heart.** The better you know it, the more spontaneous you sound.

### Plans

A plan's purpose is **action**. Open with a clear statement of purpose. Then:
1. Build a foundation of facts — only facts that bear on the action. Draw conclusions; never present facts unstrung.
2. State recommendations clearly. Consider alternatives and risks. Don't cover up problems.
3. Make it a call to action. Truman's first draft: "It is essential to our security that we assist free peoples..." → Final: *"I believe that it must be the policy of the United States to give support to free peoples..."*

### Reports

A standard structure that works:
- **Purpose** — why the reader should care
- **Summary** — no surprise endings
- **Findings** — facts (the five Ws: who, what, when, where, why)
- **Conclusions** — patterns
- **Recommendations** — proposed action
- **Next steps** — costs, timing, open issues

Separate **opinion from fact** explicitly. "It's twenty-four degrees" is fact; "It's a pleasant winter day" is opinion. Conclusions and recommendations are always opinion.

### Recommendations and proposals

Treat it as **selling, not presenting.** You're competing for finite resources.
1. Title promises a benefit.
2. First paragraph establishes subject *and* scope. For long pieces, lead with an executive summary.
3. Use numbered/bulleted headings to walk the reader through.
4. **Recommend early.** This isn't a mystery. "We propose..." in the first paragraph or two.
5. Specifics persuade. A chain of specifics is only as strong as its weakest link — cut weak ones.
6. Anticipate objections. Sometimes reframe the question: *"Does anybody read long copy?"* → *"Does long copy sell?"*
7. Emphasise benefits. Answer the reader's silent question: *"What's in it for me?"*

### Grant proposals

1. Get to the point fast — many readers want to know how much money and what for in the first paragraph.
2. Show how your project meets *their* published goals and criteria.
3. Organise to persuade — claims supported by evidence, every fact relevant.
4. Make it urgent — "the project may not happen without this funding."
5. Improve the product, not just the proposal: typos and spelling mistakes in a proposal from an educational program will sink it.
6. It need not be dull. Open with an arresting image when you can.

### Sales and fund-raising letters

- **Test.** Test envelope copy, lengths, offers, prices, send dates — but only one variable at a time.
- **Long letters often outperform short ones** if they grab attention up front, load with relevant facts, and have an attractive offer. Don't assume nobody will read a long letter.
- **Get the envelope opened.** Tease, hint, promise. "ADVANCE NOTICE." "PLEASE OPEN AT ONCE." "WE HAVE A FREE GIFT FOR YOU."
- **Find the audience first, then the message.** Pick the single most important benefit and sacrifice the others.
- **Make it inviting to read** — visual devices, headings, handwritten notes, unusual envelope sizes.
- **Create urgency.** Subscribe NOW. Deadline. Limited offer.
- **Use the P.S.** — one of the best-read parts of any letter. Restate the offer, add urgency, mention a premium.
- **Fund-raising specifics:** thank, then ask. Tell prospects how much you want. Personalise — people give to people, not institutions. State what's at risk if they don't give.

### Resumes and cover letters

**Resume**: telegraphic style, third person. "Managed 64-person department" not "I managed." No typos, ever. No photographs. Give the full names of companies/associations. Show it to someone who'll ask "So what?" and "Is this what you meant?"

**Cover letter**: never send a resume without one.
1. Think about the reader. Don't open with "I." Open with a benefit to the employer.
2. State the job you want, immediately.
3. Pique interest (a referral, a specific reason this employer).
4. Address an individual by name — never "Attention: HR Director."
5. Spell names right, double-check.
6. Be specific and factual about qualifications. Offer evidence.
7. Personal, direct, natural — like you'd say it across a desk.
8. Propose a specific next step: "I'll call your office Wednesday afternoon to see if you'd like me to come in." Don't make the employer chase you.
9. Send different letters tailored to different readers. Send lots. Follow up after a few weeks.

## Part 3 — Editing (do this on everything)

Never send the first draft of anything important. Edit to:
- Shorten
- Sharpen and clarify
- Simplify
- Check accuracy
- Improve order
- Make sure nothing is left out
- Review tone
- Examine from the reader's point of view

**First pass — one question only: *What can I cut?*** Mark Twain: "Strike out every third word — you have no idea what vigor it adds to style."

**Second pass — questions:**
1. Am I mumbling? Have I chosen verbs and adjectives that express meaning precisely?
2. Is it in the best order?
3. Are there holes in the argument? Don't make the reader leap like a goat.
4. Are the facts right? Check all statistics and quotations.
5. Is the tone right? Too stiff? Too chummy? Rude?

**Two simple editing tips:**
- **Let time elapse between drafts.** Set it aside overnight; imperfections will pop out in the morning.
- **Get another set of eyes.** David Ogilvy sent drafts with a handwritten *Please Improve.* Nearly everyone will spot at least one thing.

**A before/after example from the book:**

| First draft | Second draft |
|---|---|
| Consumer perception of the brand changed very positively. | The opinion of consumers improved. |
| Generate promotion interest through high levels of advertising spending. | Advertise heavily to build interest in promotions. |
| Using the resources of our organization in Europe, in addition to our Chicago office, we have been able to provide management with alternatives they had previously been unaware of. | Our offices in Europe and Chicago produced alternatives that management hadn't known about. |

## Part 4 — Making it easy to read (formatting)

1. **Start with a heading** in caps, top-centre. Orients the reader at once.
2. **Keep paragraphs short.** Long paragraph? Break it.
3. **Italics for emphasis**, not boldface or underlines or ALL CAPS. (All caps = SHOUTING and hurts readership.) Italics in continuous underline, not choppy word-by-word.
4. **Numbered, lettered, or bulleted points** for steps and lists. Hang the numbers in the margin.
5. **Don't over-emphasise.** When everything is emphasised, nothing is.
6. **Break up large masses of type** with subheads.
7. **Space between paragraphs**, not indents. Single spacing within, double between. Triple-space drafts you want comments on.
8. **Numbers consistently.** Spell out ten and under, numerals for 11 and up (Newspaper style). $60 million reads more easily than $60,000,000.
9. **Charts in an appendix** if they break flow. Number appendices, tab them.
10. **Number every page**, including drafts.
11. **Ragged right margin**, not justified — word-processor justification spaces words badly.
12. **Conservative fonts.** Serif for body text (more readable than sans serif); pick one and stick to it.

## How to apply this skill

When the user asks for business writing help:

1. **Identify the format** (email, memo, deck, proposal, etc.) and load the relevant Part-2 playbook *plus* the universal Part-1 principles.
2. **Ask the first question** if it isn't already answered in the user's prompt: *what do you want the reader to do?* If the user has supplied a draft, infer this from the content — don't interrogate them.
3. **Draft or edit** applying the principles. Be concrete: when you cut a phrase, the new version should be shorter and more direct, not just rearranged.
4. **Flag the specific principles** you applied if the user asked for an edit — they're learning to do this themselves. E.g. *"Cut 'advance planning' to 'planning' (Principle 12). Replaced 'utilize' with 'use' (Principle 3)."*
5. **Don't over-edit voice out.** The reader should still hear the user, just more clearly. Match register to audience: a memo to a colleague isn't a letter to a senator.

The point of every principle is the same: **respect the reader's time, and make it easy to say yes.**
